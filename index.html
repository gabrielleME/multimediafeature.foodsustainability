Food Sustainability is on the Menu

With food waste volumes reaching national crisis level, community food co-ops might just hold the solution.

Gabrielle Meredith-Elsworth visits Morecambe Sustainability

We’re all guilty of throwing food away. Those leftovers that never did get finished. Those apples that have lost their crunch. For me it’s the two for one buys that seemed like a good idea at the time but just end up sitting in the fridge, withering past their best before date. But the average household is losing £470 by the 7 million tonnes of food that is thrown away each year. Add this to the shocking statistics about food poverty – a Unicef report found that nearly one in five children in the UK under the age of 15 do not have secure access to enough food- and there is a big problem to digest.

Compared to other countries, the UK is falling behind. In 2016, France made it illegal for supermarkets to throw away food that is edible or that could be used for animal feed. A Norwegian based online platform, bestfør.no, allows supermarkets to track products’ sell by dates and alerts charities to food that cannot be sold but that is still safe to eat and has full approval from the government. A food waste inquiry was commissioned by the UK government in 2016 but there is yet to be an overall change to legislation.

There is a home-grown food sustainability movement however, thanks to willing volunteers across the country that recognise change is needed. Many cities now have cafes that turn edible food that would otherwise be thrown away into wonderful three course meals, with a pay-as-you-feel price tag. Another way food sustainability is being promoted across the UK is through organisations called ‘food co-operatives’, or food co-ops for short.These food co-ops are non-profit enterprises with the aim to save food waste and give affordable food to the community, a solution to both the aforementioned issues. I attended Sustainability Morecambe’s food club in Lancashire to see this food story for myself.

Sustainability Morecambe

“The first thing to say is that we are not a food bank,” Jo Ainscough, the Secretary of Sustainability Morecambe. “We’re a food club that reduces social isolation in the community by bringing people together, all whilst saving food from landfill”.

I ask Jo if the food bank label is a problem: “There’s a real stigma attached but in reality all types of people are members of our club, and that’s what we try to promote. We’re not just giving food to people that are short on money, we’re showing people that a lot of food that gets thrown away is of a high quality.”

This particular group was set up at the end of 2016 by a small number of people living in Morecambe that wanted to change the current attitude towards food waste. In their first year they’ve gained over fifty members, had a visit from Labour leader Jeremy Corbyn –although the group is clear that it is not apolitical- and have even won a regional award last September.

“It was just great to be shortlisted for the Bay Living Awards, but then to win best Community Focus/ Social Action really made all the hard work we do worthwhile,” explained Linda Smalley, one of the founding members of Sustainability Morecambe.
